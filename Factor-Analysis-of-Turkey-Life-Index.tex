Analysis}\label{suitability-of-the-data-for-factor-analysis}

Before conducting factor analysis, the suitability of the data set was
examined using the Kaiser--Meyer--Olkin (KMO) measure of sampling
adequacy and Bartlett's Test of Sphericity. The KMO value was found to
be 0.78, which exceeds the commonly accepted threshold of 0.60. This
result indicates that the sample size and correlation structure of the
variables are adequate for factor analysis. Bartlett's Test of
Sphericity was statistically significant (p \textless{} 0.001),
rejecting the null hypothesis that the correlation matrix is an identity
matrix. This confirms the presence of sufficient correlations among
variables and supports the application of factor analysis.

\subsection{Determination of the Number of
Factors}\label{determination-of-the-number-of-factors}

To determine the appropriate number of factors, a parallel analysis was
conducted. The results suggested the extraction of four factors.
However, considering the cumulative variance explained, factor
interpretability, and parsimony principles commonly applied in social
sciences, a three-factor solution was retained. The three-factor model
provides a clear and meaningful structure while explaining a substantial
proportion of the total variance.

\subsection{Factor Extraction and
Rotation}\label{factor-extraction-and-rotation}

Factor analysis was performed using the maximum likelihood estimation
method, and Varimax rotation was applied to enhance interpretability by
achieving a simpler and more distinct factor structure. The three
extracted factors together explain approximately 55\% of the total
variance, which is considered acceptable and adequate in social science
research.

\subsection{Interpretation of Factors}\label{interpretation-of-factors}

\textbf{Factor 1: Socio-Economic Living Conditions}

The first factor shows high loadings on variables related to housing
conditions, income and wealth, education, safety, and overall life
satisfaction. These variables collectively reflect the economic and
material foundations of quality of life. Therefore, the first factor is
interpreted as ``Socio-Economic Living Conditions.''

\textbf{Factor 2: Environmental and Social Infrastructure}

The second factor is primarily associated with variables related to
environmental quality, social life, and access to infrastructure
services. This factor captures the role of public services,
environmental conditions, and social amenities in shaping living
standards. Accordingly, the second factor is labeled ``Environmental and
Social Infrastructure.''

\textbf{Factor 3: Human Development and Social Participation}

The third factor includes variables such as work life, education, civic
engagement, and access to infrastructure. These variables emphasize
participation in social and economic life as well as individual
development opportunities. Thus, the third factor is interpreted as
\emph{Human Development and Social Participation.}

\subsubsection{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

The results of the factor analysis indicate that the Life Index (2015)
can be effectively summarized using three underlying dimensions. These
dimensions capture the socio-economic, environmental, and human
development aspects of quality of life across provinces.

The findings demonstrate that quality of life is a multidimensional
concept shaped not only by economic conditions but also by social
participation, environmental quality, and access to public services.

Overall, the factor analysis provides a statistically sound and
theoretically meaningful summary of the Life Index indicators,
supporting its use in regional quality-of-life comparisons and
policy-oriented evaluations.
